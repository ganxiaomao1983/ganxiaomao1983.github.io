The Night Our Wi‑Fi Felt Like a Liability

It started on a Tuesday night that should have been completely ordinary. I was half-watching a show on the couch while my daughter streamed videos on her tablet and my son played an online game, all of us sharing the same home Wi‑Fi.

My phone buzzed with an email that looked like a fraud alert. The subject line mentioned unusual activity on one of my accounts, and for a few seconds I couldn’t tell if it was real or a clever scam.

That was the moment I realized something I had been trying to ignore for years: our whole family was living online every day, but I didn’t really have a clear plan for keeping everyone safer from scams, identity theft, and all the little tricks that show up in our inboxes and messages.

Note: This story reflects one family’s experience. No security tool can guarantee that you will never encounter scams or identity theft.

A Year of Almost-Scams and Constant Worry

Once I started thinking back, I could list an entire year’s worth of close calls. There was the fake package-delivery text that I almost tapped. There was the so-called bank email that used my bank’s logo but pushed me to click a strange link.

My kids aren’t reckless, but they are curious. They love watching videos and downloading new apps, and it only takes one convincing pop-up or one sketchy download to turn into something serious. I found myself saying things like, “Just don’t click weird links,” but I knew that wasn’t a real plan.

I kept reading headlines about identity theft and online scams costing families real money and time. Each story made me feel a little more exposed, and I kept wondering, if one of us made a mistake, how long would it take before we even noticed?

Why Free Tools and One-Off Fixes Never Felt Enough

Like a lot of people, I started with free antivirus on a few devices and some basic browser extensions. They caught some obvious threats, but they were device-by-device tools and never addressed things like identity monitoring or scam texts.

At one point I added a separate identity monitoring service, but it was only for me. That meant I had one login for my laptop’s antivirus, another for a VPN app, another for identity alerts, and still no real coverage for my kids or my spouse.

Every solution felt like a patch, not a plan. I had bits of protection scattered across apps and subscriptions, but no single place to see what was happening for our family as a whole or what to do if someone’s information actually ended up in the wrong hands.

Key takeaway: Piecing together free tools and single-user services can help, but it can also leave gaps—especially if your family lives online across many devices.

Finding a Family-Level Plan Instead of Another Patch

I also liked that it was a recognizable security brand doing more than just antivirus. The idea of combining identity protection, device security, and tools to help spot scam content felt closer to the kind of plan I had in mind.
